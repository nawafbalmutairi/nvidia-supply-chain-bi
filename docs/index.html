This project addresses a fundamental tension in modern semiconductor supply chains: generative-AI demand is explosive and volatile, while capacity is constrained by upstream foundry and packaging bottlenecks. NVIDIA's H100 and H200 platforms, central to AI training workloads, sit at the intersection of this pressure.

A six-KPI Power BI dashboard was built on top of monthly demand, inventory, and supplier-lead-time data spanning two products and two regions (EMEA, North America). The dashboard surfaces 96.4% forecast accuracy, 24.3% MAPE, 42.3K total backorders against 255.8K inventory units, 57.0% capacity utilisation, a 59-day average supplier lead time, and 95.92% on-time delivery.

Underpinning the visualisations is a Soft Systems Methodology analysis — a rich picture of the ecosystem, a causal-loop diagram with four reinforcing/balancing feedback loops, and a Balanced Scorecard mapping each KPI to a strategic tier. The result is a dashboard that doesn't just report numbers — it explains why they move.

Introduction.

The 2023–2025 generative-AI surge transformed NVIDIA from a graphics-chip company into the backbone of the global AI training economy. H100 and H200 became scarce strategic resources. But the visible scarcity — backorders, allocation politics, billion-dollar reservation queues — is the symptom, not the system. The system is the supply chain.

This project asks: can a single BI dashboard make the underlying supply-chain dynamics legible to a non-technical operator? Six KPIs, two product lines, two regions — surfaced in a way that explains not just what is happening, but why.

Research questions.

  • What are the right six headline KPIs for an AI-GPU supply chain at this scale?
  • How should these KPIs be connected to a strategic narrative — not just listed as numbers?
  • What feedback dynamics drive the observed patterns, and which loops are reinforcing vs balancing?

Methodology · SSM & Balanced Scorecard.

The methodology has three layers stacked on top of each other: a rich picture of the ecosystem (who's involved and what flows between them), a causal-loop diagram showing how the feedback works, and a Balanced Scorecard mapping the KPIs to a strategic layer-cake. The dashboard sits on top of all three.

Layer 1 — Rich Picture.

A rich picture is an SSM technique that maps an ecosystem informally — actors, flows, and pressures, all in a single visual. For NVIDIA's AI-GPU supply chain, the rich picture identifies:

  • NVIDIA Operations & Planning — the actor whose decisions the dashboard supports.
  • TSMC (advanced foundry) and CoWoS (packaging) — capacity-constrained, hard to scale on a quarterly timeline.
  • Cloud hyperscalers (AWS, Azure, GCP), enterprise customers, OEMs, and logistics providers.
  • Competitors, geopolitics & export controls, market expectations driven by quarterly cycles.
  • Generative-AI demand — explosive, volatile, the root cause of everything else.
  • Backorders / unserved demand — the visible failure mode that everything else feeds into.

Layer 2 — Causal Loop Diagram.

Four feedback loops drive the observable behaviour of the system:

  • Demand Surge (reinforcing). AI demand ↑ → backorders ↑ → customer pressure ↑ → production push ↑ → capacity strain ↑ → lead times ↑ → backorders ↑ (and the loop continues).
  • Forecast Volatility (reinforcing). Volatile orders → larger forecast errors → unstable inventory positions → more reactive ordering → further volatility.
  • Capacity Constraint (balancing). Production limits cap absolute throughput; eventually unmet demand forces forecast revision downward, stabilising the system.
  • Lead Time Adjustment (balancing). Long lead times → delayed shipments → planning teams add buffer → smoother future cycles.

Layer 3 — Balanced Scorecard.

The Balanced Scorecard maps each KPI to one of four strategic tiers, so the dashboard reads as a strategy artefact rather than a list of metrics:

  • Learning & Growth — capability foundation: BI & data skills, forecasting expertise, systems thinking, tool fluency.
  • Internal Processes — engine improvements: reduce forecast error (MAPE), tighten supply planning, resolve CoWoS bottlenecks, balance capacity.
  • Customer — loyalty: shorter lead times, higher on-time delivery, fewer unserved orders, confidence in supply commitments.
  • Financial — result: predictable revenue, lower cost of delays, minimised lost sales, stable margin profile.

Data & Model.

The dashboard is powered by a star-schema data model with one fact table and three dimension tables. The fact table contains monthly demand observations — forecast and actual — joined to Product (H100 / H200) and Region (EMEA / NA) dimensions, plus a date dimension supporting time-intelligence DAX measures.

Data scope.

  • Time span: 12 months of monthly observations.
  • Products: 2 (H100, H200).
  • Regions: 2 (EMEA, North America).
  • Tables: Demand fact + Product / Region / Date dimensions.
  • Inventory / backorder snapshots: point-in-time, by region.
i
The data is synthetic / illustrative. NVIDIA does not publish operational supply-chain data at this granularity. The values used are designed to represent plausible AI-GPU supply-chain dynamics so the methodology and visualisations are evaluable on their own terms.

Dashboard & KPIs.

The dashboard is a single-page Power BI artefact with six KPI cards across the top, three chart panels in the middle (lead time, forecast vs actual, inventory table), and a 12-month production-vs-demand bar chart along the bottom. Two slicers (Product, Region) cross-filter every visual.

DAX Measures.

Eight DAX measures power the dashboard. The three most consequential:

MEASURE 01 · Forecast Accuracy
Forecast Accuracy = 1 - DIVIDE( SUMX( Demand, ABS( Demand[Forecast] - Demand[Actual] ) ), SUMX( Demand, Demand[Actual] ) )
MEASURE 02 · MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error)
MAPE = AVERAGEX( Demand, DIVIDE( ABS( Demand[Forecast] - Demand[Actual] ), Demand[Actual] ) )
MEASURE 03 · 3-Month Moving Average of Actual Demand
3M Moving Avg Demand = CALCULATE( AVERAGE( Demand[Actual] ), DATESINPERIOD( Demand[MonthEnd], LASTDATE( Demand[MonthEnd] ), -3, MONTH ) )

The 3-month moving average is overlaid on the forecast-vs-actual line chart. It smooths short-term noise so the underlying demand trend is legible, separately from month-to-month variance.

Discussion & Insights.

The May/November pattern.

The 3-month moving-average overlay revealed a recurring May/November under-shoot: forecasts consistently overstated actuals in those two months. The most plausible explanation is enterprise budget calendars — large customers defer orders ahead of fiscal-year-end (May for many UK/EU FYs, November for US calendar year-end deferrals to next-year capex). The recommendation: add a categorical fiscal-quarter-end feature to the forecast model.

Why 57% capacity utilisation is suspicious

A 57.0% utilisation alongside 42.3K backorders and 95.92% on-time delivery should not coexist. If capacity were really at 57%, the dashboard would show excess capacity sitting idle while backorders accumulate — which is a contradiction.

The likely explanation: capacity utilisation is mismeasured. The metric is probably reporting NVIDIA's own assembly capacity, not the bottleneck — which is upstream CoWoS packaging. CoWoS likely runs at 95%+, and the dashboard's capacity metric is misleading the operator about where the constraint lives.

What the causal loops predict.

The reinforcing loops (R1, R2) explain why the situation feels unstable: demand and backorders amplify each other until a balancing loop kicks in. The balancing loops (B1, B2) are slow — capacity expansion takes years, not quarters. So in the short term, R1 and R2 dominate, and the system stays under stress. The dashboard makes this story visible.

Limitations & Future Work.

  • Synthetic data. Without access to real NVIDIA operational data, the absolute values are illustrative. The methodology, however, transfers directly.
  • 12-month window. A multi-year window would surface yearly seasonality and competitive-launch effects (e.g. AMD MI300X, Intel Gaudi).
  • Static visuals. A real-time refresh (Power BI Service + DirectQuery) would enable operational use; the current artefact is a snapshot.
  • No supplier dimension. Future iterations should add CoWoS, HBM3 memory, and substrate suppliers as separate dimensions to localise the bottleneck inside the dashboard itself.

Conclusion.

The dashboard answers RQ1 (six headline KPIs) by surfacing forecast accuracy, MAPE, backorders, capacity utilisation, lead time, and on-time delivery — each chosen because it corresponds to one Balanced Scorecard tier. It answers RQ2 (strategic narrative) by stacking the rich picture, causal loop diagram, and Balanced Scorecard underneath the KPIs, so every metric on the dashboard maps back to a why. And it answers RQ3 (feedback dynamics) by identifying two reinforcing loops driving instability and two balancing loops moving too slowly to compensate in the short term.

The headline finding is the even split of backorders across EMEA and NA — the bottleneck is structural and upstream, not regional. The recommendation is therefore not better allocation but more total supply, which requires investment in CoWoS packaging capacity. That single insight, derived from a dashboard plus a causal-loop diagram, is worth more than any single KPI on its own.
